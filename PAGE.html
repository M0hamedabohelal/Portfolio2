Project Brief

The Arkan Research Center is conceived as a pioneering facility that bridges the gap between advanced scientific research and the built environment. Located in a semi-arid context, the project addresses the need for a collaborative, future-ready research campus that prioritizes both environmental performance and human well-being.


The program encompasses specialized laboratories, flexible co-working zones, seminar rooms, administrative offices, and public exhibition areas — all organized around a central atrium that acts as the spatial and social heart of the complex.

Project 01 — Arkan Research Center

Concept & Vision

03

Design Concept

The conceptual framework draws from the notion of "permeable boundaries" — the idea that a research center should not isolate its occupants but rather create a continuous dialogue between interior program and exterior landscape. The building's massing is carved and fragmented to allow natural light, ventilation, and visual connections to penetrate deep into the plan.


Structural rhythm and material honesty serve as the primary design drivers. Exposed concrete, weathered steel, and floor-to-ceiling glazing establish a tactile and visual language that reflects the precision of scientific inquiry while maintaining warmth and spatial generosity.

Architectural Vision

The vision for Arkan Research Center transcends conventional laboratory design. It aspires to create an environment where architecture itself becomes an instrument of discovery — where spatial qualities such as light, proportion, and materiality stimulate creative thinking and intellectual exchange.


The interplay of solid and void, transparency and opacity, establishes a dynamic architectural narrative that evolves as occupants move through the building. Each space is calibrated to its specific function while contributing to the holistic experience of the complex.

Site Context

The site is situated within an emerging academic district, characterized by low-to-medium density institutional buildings and open landscape buffers. The terrain is predominantly flat with a gentle topographic shift of 2.5 meters across the site from north to south, informing natural drainage strategies and sectional design decisions.

Key Site Parameters

  • Site area: 8,200 m²
  • Zoning: Institutional / Research
  • Setbacks: Front 10m, Sides 5m, Rear 8m
  • Max height: 18m
  • FAR: 1.5
  • Primary access: Southern road
  • Climate: Semi-arid, hot summers, mild winters
  • Prevailing wind: Northwest
Strengths
  • Prominent location within academic precinct
  • Generous site area allowing spatial articulation
  • Strong solar orientation potential for passive strategies
  • Proximity to university infrastructure and talent pool
Weaknesses
  • Harsh summer climate demanding extensive shading
  • Limited public transport connectivity
  • Flat terrain offering minimal natural character
  • Adjacent undeveloped parcels creating uncertainty
Opportunities
  • Establish a precedent for sustainable research architecture
  • Leverage topographic shift for semi-basement strategies
  • Create a public research hub engaging the community
  • Integrate renewable energy as a visible design feature
Threats
  • Future adjacent development may compromise views and light
  • Budget constraints limiting material and system aspirations
  • Dust and sand exposure affecting façade longevity
  • Evolving research needs requiring long-term flexibility

Design Development

05

Design Development — Interior Atrium View

Central void connecting all levels with controlled natural light

Spatial Organization

The plan is structured around a longitudinal atrium that bisects the building mass, creating two interconnected wings. The eastern wing houses public and administrative functions, while the western wing accommodates research laboratories and controlled environments.

Circulation Strategy

A primary circulation spine runs along the atrium, with secondary corridors branching into each wing. Vertical circulation is concentrated at three nodes — the main entrance lobby, a mid-point core, and a service core — ensuring efficient movement and clear wayfinding.

Environmental Response

The southern façade employs a brise-soleil system with horizontal louvers calibrated to solar angles, reducing heat gain by 40% while preserving views. The northern elevation maximizes glazing for diffuse daylight. Green roofs and permeable paving manage stormwater on-site.

Project Brief

The Villa Project is a contemporary private residence designed for a family of five. Located on a 1,200 m² parcel within a suburban residential district, the design brief called for a home that balances privacy with openness, formality with domestic warmth, and architectural ambition with everyday livability.


The program includes formal and informal living areas, a kitchen with ancillary spaces, five bedrooms with en-suite bathrooms, a home office, a gym, and outdoor amenities including a pool and landscaped garden. The design approach privileges spatial flow, natural light, and a strong connection between interior and exterior realms.

Project 02 — Villa Project

Concept & Layout

10

Design Concept

The design concept is rooted in the idea of "layered privacy" — a spatial progression from the most public zone at the entrance to the most private sanctuaries at the rear of the house. This gradient is expressed architecturally through a series of interlocking volumes that step down in height and shift in plan, creating intimate courtyards and sheltered terraces at each transition.


The material palette — board-formed concrete, warm timber, and floor-to-ceiling glass — establishes a language of restrained luxury. Each material is allowed to age naturally, deepening in character over time and connecting the house to the passage of seasons.

Spatial Layout (1:100)

The ground floor operates on an open-plan principle, with the living, dining, and kitchen areas arranged in an L-shape around a central courtyard. This courtyard, visible from all primary spaces, serves as the visual and spiritual anchor of the house — a meditative garden that brings light, air, and nature into the daily experience of the occupants.


The upper floor is organized as a private wing, with bedrooms accessed via a gallery overlooking the double-height living space below. The master suite occupies the full width of the eastern end, with a private terrace framing distant views.

Wall Assembly

The external wall system comprises 200mm board-formed concrete with an internal layer of 50mm rigid insulation, a ventilated cavity, and 12mm internal plaster finish. This assembly achieves a U-value of 0.28 W/m²K, exceeding local energy code requirements. The concrete formwork pattern is deliberately retained as a finish, eliminating the need for additional cladding and reducing material waste.

Glazing System

Floor-to-ceiling glazing employs triple-pane low-E units with argon fill, achieving a U-value of 1.1 W/m²K and a solar heat gain coefficient of 0.25. Frames are thermally broken aluminum in a dark bronze anodized finish, selected for durability and minimal visual weight. Motorized external blinds provide additional solar control on western exposures.
